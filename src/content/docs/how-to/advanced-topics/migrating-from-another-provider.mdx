Switching provider can feel like the riskiest thing you will do with your
portfolio — these are live domains carrying real traffic and email. The good
news is that a domain migration is, at its core, a sequence of ordinary transfers
you already know how to run, done in a planned order with the safety nets in
place. Handled well, your domains keep resolving throughout and your customers
never notice.

This guide gives you a provider-agnostic framework, short notes for the providers
resellers most often come from, and — above all — the risk mitigations that keep
every domain live from start to finish.

:::tip[By the end of this guide you will have]
- A clear **migration framework** you can apply to any source provider
- A plan to **export your domains and auth codes** from your current provider
- A **bulk transfer strategy** with the pre-checks that prevent rejections
- The **risk mitigations** that keep domains resolving throughout the cutover
- A realistic sense of **timeline** and where to get help for large portfolios
:::

:::note[First things first]
A migration lands into a working CentralNic Reseller account. If you have not set
one up yet, start with [Your First Day as a CentralNic Reseller](../../start-here/your-first-day/)
so your account is funded and secured before any domains arrive.
:::

## How a domain migration works

A migration is a set of **inbound transfers**: you move each domain's sponsoring
registrar from your current provider to CentralNic Reseller. Two facts make this
far less scary than it sounds:

- **Domains stay live during a transfer.** A transfer changes who manages the
  domain — it does not, by itself, change where the domain resolves. As long as
  the nameservers keep answering, the website and email keep working throughout.
- **A transfer usually adds a year.** For most gTLDs an inbound transfer includes
  a one-year renewal, so migrating also extends your registrations.

The mechanics of a single transfer in — auth codes, eligibility, status tracking
and failure handling — are covered in depth in
[Transfer a Domain into CentralNic Reseller](../../working-with-domains/transfer-a-domain-in/).
This page is about doing that at portfolio scale, safely.

## The migration framework

The same six steps apply whatever you are migrating from. Work through them in
order — the preparation steps are what turn a nerve-wracking cutover into a
routine one.

<Steps>

1. **Inventory and export.** Export the full list of domains from your current
   provider, along with their expiry dates and current nameservers. You will use
   this as your migration checklist and your before/after comparison.

2. **Prepare each domain at the source.** For every domain, at your current
   provider: unlock it (remove `clientTransferProhibited`), disable WHOIS/domain
   privacy if it blocks the transfer, confirm the administrative contact email is
   current (transfer approvals are often sent there), and retrieve the
   **authorisation ("auth" or EPP) code**.

3. **Recreate DNS and contacts in CentralNic Reseller first.** Before you move
   anything, set up your [DNS zones](../../working-with-domains/dns-automation/)
   and [contact handles](../../working-with-domains/contacts-handles-ownership/)
   in CentralNic Reseller so they are ready and waiting. This is the single most
   important risk mitigation — see below.

4. **Submit the transfers.** Start the inbound transfers, in batches you can
   monitor. Begin with a small pilot batch to confirm your process end to end,
   then scale up.

5. **Monitor to completion.** Track each transfer's status through to completion
   and watch for the ones that need action (an approval, a corrected email, a
   removed lock). Handle rejections as they surface rather than at the end.

6. **Verify after cutover.** Reconcile the transferred domains against your
   inventory, confirm nameservers and contacts are correct, and set your
   [renewal modes](../../working-with-domains/complete-domain-lifecycle/) so the
   portfolio behaves the way you expect going forward.

</Steps>

## Keeping every domain live throughout

The one outcome to avoid is a domain going dark mid-migration. Three rules
prevent it:

:::caution[Replicate DNS before you transfer — not after]
If your domains currently use your old provider's nameservers, their resolution
depends on a provider you are leaving. **Recreate the DNS zones in CentralNic
Reseller and cut nameservers over first**, or keep the existing authoritative
nameservers in place until the transfer completes. Never let a domain's only
working DNS live at the registrar you are moving away from.
:::

:::caution[Mind expiry dates and the transfer window]
Do not transfer a domain that is about to expire — renew it at the source first,
or you risk it lapsing mid-transfer. Also note that many gTLDs enforce a
**60-day lock** after a registration or a previous transfer, during which a domain
cannot be transferred again. This is registry/ICANN policy and varies by TLD, so
check eligibility per domain (your inventory export makes this easy to spot).
:::

:::tip[Pilot, then scale]
Run a small first batch — a handful of low-risk domains — all the way through
before committing your whole portfolio. It validates your export, your auth codes
and your monitoring in miniature, so the bulk run holds no surprises.
:::

## Provider-specific notes

Every major provider lets you export your domains and retrieve auth codes — the
details differ, but the shape is the same. Use your current provider's own
documentation for the exact clicks; the notes below are just where to look.

- **Openprovider** — export your domain list from the control panel or via their
  API, unlock domains and pull auth codes per domain. Openprovider resellers often
  already run EPP, which can make the CentralNic Reseller side familiar — see the
  [EPP Integration Guide](../../building-your-integration/epp-integration-guide/).
- **OpenSRS (Tucows)** — retrieve your domain list and auth codes from the OpenSRS
  management console or API, and check that domain locking and privacy are cleared
  before requesting each transfer.
- **Enom** — export domains and obtain auth (EPP) codes from the Enom account,
  unlock domains, and confirm the admin contact email so transfer approvals reach
  you.
- **ResellerClub** — export your domain list and auth codes from the reseller
  control panel, disable privacy protection where enabled, and unlock before
  transferring.

:::caution[Bring the data, not the documentation]
Export **your own** domain data — lists, auth codes, contact and DNS records — from
whichever provider you are leaving. Do not rely on copied third-party
instructions here; each provider maintains its own current export steps, and
those are the authoritative source for their side of the move.
:::

## Migrating a large portfolio

For a handful of domains, the self-service framework above is all you need. For a
large portfolio, the transfer volume, sequencing and reconciliation are worth
planning carefully — and you may not have to do it alone.

:::danger[Internal source — please review]
CentralNic Reseller appears to offer **assisted / managed migration** for larger
portfolios — an internal 2021 **draft** process ("Domain Migration Mapping",
Confluence page 838395437) describes a Migration Project Manager, a data-intake
step, pre-transfer checks, a coordinated bulk transfer and a post-migration
report, and a related "Migration documents" page (6452609032) holds a
bulk registrar-to-registrar transfer template. None of this is published in the
public knowledge base and it is a draft, so the exact offering, process, timeline
and any SLA must be confirmed with the CentralNic Reseller team before it is
stated to customers. Based on: internal, unconfirmed — confirm with your account
manager / the migrations team.
:::

Whatever assistance is available, the self-service mechanics are the same
underneath, so preparing your export, auth codes, DNS and contacts as described
above is never wasted effort.

## A realistic timeline

Plan around the parts you do not control. Preparation (export, unlock, auth codes,
recreating DNS and contacts) is entirely in your hands and can be done ahead of
time. The transfers themselves are **asynchronous**: each inbound transfer can take
up to several days to complete, depending on the TLD and how quickly the losing
registrar releases it, and some registries wait out an acknowledgement period. So
schedule the *submission* as a short window, but expect *completion* to trail over
days — and keep DNS live throughout, which is exactly why the DNS-first rule above
matters.
