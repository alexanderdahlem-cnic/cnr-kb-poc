Every domain carries a small cast of **contacts** — the people or organisations
behind it — and getting that model right on the first attempt saves a great deal
of rework later. Contacts are one of the most common onboarding stumbling
blocks: it is easy to treat them as an afterthought, then hit a registry
validation error at registration time and have to refactor. This guide walks you
through how CentralNic Reseller thinks about contacts so you can design your data
model once and trust it.

The core idea is simple. A contact is a reusable object with its own ID — a
**handle** — that you create once and then attach to as many domains as you like.
A domain does not store contact details directly; it *references* handles for
each role it needs. Understand handles, and everything else falls into place.

:::tip[By the end of this page you will understand]
- The **four contact roles** — registrant (owner), admin, billing and tech — and
  what each one is for

  trade-offs either way
- What GDPR means for **the data you collect versus what appears in public WHOIS/RDDS**
- Why some **TLDs demand particular contact data**, and how to plan for it
:::

## The four contact roles

Every domain registration recognises four contact roles. Whether a given TLD
requires all four or just the registrant, the roles always mean the same thing:

| Role | CentralNic Reseller term | What it represents |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Registrant** | Owner | The legal holder of the domain — the person or organisation that *owns* it. This is the contact that carries legal weight. |
| **Administrative** | Admin | The person authorised to manage the domain and make decisions about it on the registrant's behalf. |
| **Technical** | Tech | The contact responsible for the technical operation — DNS, nameservers and the like. |
| **Billing** | Billing | The contact responsible for payment and renewal matters. |

:::note[Registrant and "Owner" are the same thing]
Across the wider industry the legal holder is called the **registrant**; in the
CentralNic Reseller Control Panel and API that role is labelled **Owner**. When
you see `OWNER` in a command or response, read it as "registrant". The other
three roles keep their familiar names.
:::

Of the four, the **registrant/owner is special**. The admin, tech and billing
contacts can be updated with an ordinary contact change. The registrant cannot:
changing who *owns* a domain is a distinct operation — an **owner change** (often
called a trade) — because it transfers legal control. Depending on the TLD it can
involve a confirmation exchange, and sometimes a fee or identity documents. Model
the registrant with that in mind from the start. See
[ICANN Owner Change](../../../domains/icann/icann-ownerchange/) for how the
process works across gTLDs.

## How contacts are represented: handles

CentralNic Reseller stores each contact as a first-class object identified by a
**handle** — its contact ID. You create the contact once, the system returns a
handle, and from then on you reference that handle from any domain that needs it.
A single handle can serve as Owner, Admin, Tech and Billing all at once, or you
can assign a different handle to each role.

When you supply the data for a natural person — a first, (optional) middle and
last name together with address details — the system creates a **P-Handle** in
the form `P-WWW####`, where each `W` is an uppercase letter (A–Z) and each `#` is
a digit (0–9), for example `P-ABC123`. This is the kind of handle you want.

:::caution[Let the system make a P-Handle, not an O-Handle]
If you *do not* provide the data of a named natural person, our system generates
a so-called **O-Handle** instead. We strongly recommend always creating proper
**P-Handles** with real personal or organisational data. See
[Managing Contacts](../../../domains/contact-handles/) for the full explanation.
:::

To use the *same* handle for every role you simply reference it four times. To use
*different* handles per role, create each contact separately (one per handle)
first, then reference them. Full guidance and the related API commands are on the
[Managing Contacts](../../../domains/contact-handles/) page; over EPP the same
objects are created and referenced as described in the
[EPP integration guide](../../building-your-integration/epp-integration-guide/#contact-objects-and-mapping).

## Reuse an existing handle, or create a new one?

This is the decision that trips people up. Because a handle is a shared object,
**reusing one propagates**: update a reused handle and the change reaches every
domain and every role it is attached to. That is a feature when the contacts are
genuinely the same, and a hazard when they are not.

Use this to decide:

<Steps>

1. **Same real-world entity, many domains?** Reuse one handle. A single registrant
   who owns a hundred domains should be one handle referenced a hundred times —
   one place to keep the address current, and one contact that stays **LINKED**
   (see the caution below).

2. **Same entity, all four roles on one domain?** Reuse one handle across the
   roles unless the TLD needs them to differ. Fewer objects, less to maintain.

3. **Different real people or organisations?** Create separate handles. Never
   share a handle between two entities just to save a step — a later update to one
   would silently rewrite the other, and disentangling them after the fact is
   painful.

4. **A one-off or short-lived contact?** A new handle is fine, but know that
   unused handles do not linger forever (see below).

</Steps>

:::caution[Unused handles are deleted automatically]
A contact that has not been linked to any product (domain, certificate, and so
on) for **120 days** is deleted automatically. Reused handles that stay attached
to live domains are safe; orphaned handles are not. You can exempt a handle with
the `AUTODELETE` parameter, check whether a handle is `LINKED` or `UNLINKED`, and
recover a deleted one — all covered in
[Automated Contact Deletion](../../../domains/contact-handles/automated-contact-deletion/).
:::

:::caution[Avoid generic "role" contacts]
It is tempting to create a shared contact such as "Domain Administrator" or
"Hostmaster" and reuse it everywhere. We advise against it unless a specific
registry requires or accepts one. Generic role contacts cause problems later in a
domain's life — for example, an owner change may require identity documents that
simply do not exist for a fictitious "role", blocking the operation. Prefer real,
named contacts.
:::

## GDPR: what you collect versus what is published

Here is the distinction that matters most, because the two things are separate:

- **What you collect** — CentralNic Reseller still needs *full, accurate* contact
  data. That obligation has not gone away under GDPR: the data is required for our
  own legitimate business purposes and for the data controllers we work under,
  such as ICANN and the registries. Always provide complete, correct details.
- **What is published** — by **default we do not disclose personal data in WHOIS**.
  Contact fields transmitted to registries and returned by our WHOIS server are
  **redacted or replaced** (for example with `REDACTED FOR PRIVACY`), and the
  contact email is replaced with a generic address so people can still make contact
  through [domain-contact.org](../../../help/gdpr/contact-to-an-undisclosed-entity/).

In other words: collect everything, publish nothing by default. The background,
benefits and scope of this are on the
[General Data Protection Regulation](../../../help/gdpr/) page, and the exact
field-by-field behaviour — thick versus thin registries, which fields are redacted
and which are replaced — is in
[Reduced publication and transmission of WHOIS data](../../../help/gdpr/reduced-publication-and-transmission-of-whois-data/).

:::tip[Disclosure is opt-in, per handle]
A contact who *wants* their details public can opt in. You trigger a disclosure
email (via the `RequestToken` command), and the contact chooses field by field —
name, organisation, address, phone, fax, email — what to reveal. Because it is
tied to the handle, the choice applies wherever that handle is used. Note that
even when a contact opts to disclose, the registry controlling the WHOIS output
may still not publish it. Full detail in
[Opt-in to Disclose Data in WHOIS](../../../domains/contact-handles/opt-in-to-disclose-data-in-whois/).
:::

WHOIS itself is part of ICANN's broader **Registration Directory Services (RDS)**
framework, which governs registrant, administrative, technical and billing contact
data for gTLDs — background is on the
[Registration Directory Services](../../../domains/icann/registration-directory-services/)
page, and you can shape your own output as described in
[Customise your WHOIS output](../../../domains/icann/registration-directory-services/customise-your-whois-output/).

## Registry-specific contact requirements

The four roles are universal, but the **data each TLD demands is not**. What one
registry accepts, another rejects — and these rules are **TLD-dependent**, so
always check the requirements of the specific TLD before you build. A few patterns
to plan for:

- **Extra fields for certain TLDs.** Some registries require additional contact
  data beyond the standard set — for instance a state/province, a national
  identifier or PIN, or personal details such as a birth date or city. These are
  supplied as registry-specific parameters. The
  [EPP integration guide](../../building-your-integration/epp-integration-guide/#registry-specific-data-through-extensions)
  shows how such fields are passed (for example a France or Italy extension), and
  the per-TLD pages state exactly what each registry expects.
- **Contact verification.** Many gTLDs require the registrant's email to be
  verified after registration, or the domain risks suspension. Plan for the
  verification flow rather than being surprised by it — see
  [Contact Verification](../../../domains/icann/contact-verification/).
- **Keep the organisation field empty for private individuals.** For a private
  person, leave the organisation field blank. Do **not** enter placeholder text
  such as "private" or "none": some registries treat the organisation field as the
  registrant's identity, so bogus data can end up published in WHOIS or, worse,
  cause the registry to suspend or delete the domain for invalid data.

:::caution[Check the TLD before you register, not after]
Registry validation happens at registration time. If you discover a TLD's contact
requirements only when a `Domain:Create` is rejected, you may have to rebuild your
contact model. Confirm each TLD's rules — additional fields, verification,
role-specific handles — while you are still designing, so the first registration
succeeds.
:::
