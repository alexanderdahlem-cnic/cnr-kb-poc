If you know your way around an API, the mechanics of registering a domain look
simple: send a command, get a response. But a domain is not an ordinary record you
create and delete at will. It is a **time-limited lease governed by registry
rules** â€” it passes through a defined sequence of states, some transitions are
one-way, and a few of them are surprisingly expensive to reverse. The developers
who trip up are almost never the ones who got the syntax wrong; they are the ones
who assumed a domain behaves like a row in their own database.

This page is the map of that territory. It walks the full arc â€” **check â†’ register
â†’ modify â†’ DNS â†’ transfer â†’ renew â†’ expire â†’ restore â†’ delete** â€” and explains what
each state *means*, what triggers a move from one to the next, and where the
registry, not CentralNic Reseller, sets the rules. It deliberately stays
conceptual: you will not find code here. Instead, each stage links to the
how-to or reference page that carries the concrete commands and detail, so this
becomes the guide that ties the other Working-with-Domains articles together.

:::tip[By the end of this page you will understand]
- Why a domain is a **lease with a lifecycle**, not a permanent record you own
- What each **state and status code** means, and which transitions are one-way
- How **grace periods** â€” add, auto-renew, redemption â€” change what an action costs
- Why **renewal, expiry, restore and delete** are distinct operations with
  distinct consequences (and why **restore is not re-registration**)
- Which **guide or reference** to open for the commands behind each stage
:::

:::note[Read this before you automate]
You can build a working integration by reading command references alone â€” but the

## A domain is a lease, not a purchase

Every stage below makes more sense once one idea is fixed: you never *own* a
domain outright. You hold the right to use it for a period â€” typically one to ten
years â€” from a **registry** (the operator of the TLD, such as the operator behind
`.com`), through a **registrar** (here, CentralNic Reseller). Two consequences
follow, and they drive everything else:

- **It expires.** If nothing extends the lease, the domain lapses on a schedule
  the registry defines. Renewal is not optional maintenance; it is what keeps the
  domain yours.
- **The registry has the final say.** CentralNic Reseller passes your requests to
  the registry, but each TLD sets its own rules â€” how long grace periods run,
  whether an explicit renewal is even possible, what a restore costs, and more.
  Where this page gives a specific number, it is the common gTLD case; **ccTLDs
  frequently differ**, so always confirm the individual TLD's rules.

A domain's current state is reported through **EPP status codes** â€” a standardised
vocabulary (`ok`, `pendingTransfer`, `redemptionPeriod`, and so on) that every
registry speaks. Learning to read them is the single most useful skill for
operating domains at scale; the full list lives in the
[EPP Status Codes](../../../domains/status/) reference, and we point to the
relevant ones at each stage.

## Check: is the name available?

The lifecycle begins before a domain exists, with a question: *can I have this
name?* A **check** asks the registry whether a name is available to register right
now. It changes nothing and costs nothing, which is exactly why you should always
do it first â€” it is the cheapest way to avoid a failed registration and the
fastest confirmation that your credentials and connection are working.

The API commands are `CheckDomain` (one name) and `CheckDomains` (several);
over EPP the equivalent is `Domain:Check`. A name that comes back unavailable is
not necessarily *registrable-later-for-more* â€” some names are premium-priced,
some are reserved or blocked, and some are simply already registered by someone
else. Check tells you availability; pricing and premium status are separate
lookups.

:::tip[Availability is a moment in time]
A successful check is not a hold. The name can be registered by someone else in
the seconds between your check and your create, so treat check as a pre-flight
test, not a reservation â€” and always be ready for a create to fail even after a
clean check.
:::

## Register: create the domain

Registration is where the lease is created. A **create** initiates the domain
registration at the registry (`AddDomain`, or `Domain:Create` over EPP) and, once
accepted, the domain becomes yours for the period you requested. Three things must
be in place, and each is more than a formality:

- **A registration period** â€” how many years the initial lease runs, within the
  minimum and maximum the TLD allows.
- **Nameservers** â€” where the domain's DNS is answered (see
  [DNS](#dns-making-the-domain-resolve) below). A domain with no nameservers is
  registered but does not resolve.
- **Contacts** â€” the registrant, admin, tech and billing parties, referenced by
  their **handles**. You create a contact once and reuse its handle across many
  domains. Which roles a TLD requires, and what data each needs, varies by
  registry. See [Managing Contacts](../../../domains/contact-handles/) for the
  handle model and [Understanding Contacts, Handles & Ownership](../contacts-handles-ownership/)
  for how the roles and GDPR fit together.

A freshly registered gTLD domain carries an informative `addPeriod` status for its
first several days. This **add grace period** exists so that a domain deleted
almost immediately after registration can be credited back â€” useful to know, but
not something most integrations act on directly. If the domain has no nameservers
yet, you will also see `inactive`: registered, but not resolving.

:::caution[Many TLDs need more than the basics]
Beyond period, nameservers and contacts, numerous TLDs require extra data â€”
identity documents, local-presence details, registrant validations â€” that the
base registration does not carry. A create rejected "for missing data" is usually
a TLD-specific requirement, not a bug. Full registration flows live in
[Building Your Integration](../../building-your-integration/); the authoritative
command list is the [API Command Reference](../../../api/api-commands/api-command-reference/).
:::

## Modify: change data on a live domain

Once a domain exists you will change things about it â€” nameservers, contacts,
renewal behaviour, locks. A **modify** (`ModifyDomain`, or `Domain:Update` over
EPP) updates the domain in place; the lease itself is untouched. This is the
routine, low-drama part of the lifecycle, but two ideas matter.

First, **locks are your friend, and they are status codes**. A domain can carry
`clientUpdateProhibited`, `clientTransferProhibited` and `clientDeleteProhibited`
â€” protections that make the domain reject updates, transfers or deletions until
the lock is lifted. These prevent accidental or malicious changes; the trade-off
is that your own automation must remove a lock before the operation it guards will
succeed. Their `server*` counterparts are set by the registry and take precedence.

Second, **changing the registrant is special**. Updating an admin or tech contact
is a normal modify. Changing the *owner* (registrant) is an **ownership change** â€”
a trade â€” which for many TLDs is a distinct, sometimes fee-bearing, sometimes
document-requiring operation (`TradeDomain`), not an ordinary contact update. This
is precisely why we advise against fictional "role" contacts as the registrant:
they make a later trade far harder. See
[Managing Contacts](../../../domains/contact-handles/) for why.

## DNS: making the domain resolve

Registering a domain and making it *work* are two different things. Registration
records who holds the name; **DNS** is what turns the name into an answer a browser
can use. The link between them is the domain's **nameservers** â€” the servers
authoritative for its zone.

If a domain has no nameservers associated, it shows `inactive` and will not
resolve, no matter how correctly it is registered. Conversely, a registry can set
`serverHold` (or a registrar `clientHold`) to *deliberately* stop a domain
resolving â€” used during disputes or non-payment â€” even while the registration
itself is fine. Reading those status codes tells you whether a "site is down"
problem is a DNS problem or a lifecycle problem.

You can point a domain at external nameservers, or use CentralNic Reseller's DNS
so records live alongside the domain. The zone-and-record workflows â€” creating
zones, managing records, DNSSEC, bulk changes â€” are their own topic; see
[DNS Automation with CentralNic APIs](../dns-automation/).

## Transfer: moving a domain between registrars

A domain can move from one registrar to another without changing hands or lapsing.
A **transfer** is that move. It matters here because it is **asynchronous and
multi-step** â€” unlike a register or modify, you do not get the final answer in a
single request. You initiate it (`TransferDomain`, or `Transfer:Request` over EPP)
and the outcome arrives later, once the other side and the registry have acted.

Three rules catch people out:

- **You need the auth code.** Transferring a domain *in* requires its
  authorisation ("auth" / EPP) code, obtained from the current registrar. It is
  the proof of authority that makes the transfer legitimate.
- **The domain must be transferable.** It has to be unlocked
  (`clientTransferProhibited` removed), past any registry age or
  recent-transfer restriction, and not in a state that blocks it. CentralNic
  Reseller applies a default transfer lock to newly registered and transferred-in
  domains, which is exactly this protection working as intended.
- **A completed transfer starts a grace period.** After a successful inbound
  transfer the domain carries `transferPeriod` for a limited time â€” an informative
  grace window, mirroring the add and renew grace periods elsewhere.

Some TLDs also allow contact handles to change *during* the transfer, and some do
not; the behaviour and the current list are documented in the
[Transfer](../../../domains/transfers/) reference. For the step-by-step inbound
flow â€” eligibility checks, auth codes, tracking and bulk moves â€” see
[Transfer a Domain into CentralNic Reseller](../transfer-a-domain-in/).

:::caution[Track the status; do not assume success]
Because transfers resolve over time, a request that returns cleanly means
*started*, not *done*. Watch the domain's status (`pendingTransfer`) and, over
EPP, the poll queue, until the registry confirms the result. Automation that
treats "request accepted" as "transfer complete" will get the state wrong.
:::

## Renew: extending the lease

Renewal is how you extend the lease before it runs out â€” and it is where
CentralNic Reseller's model most rewards understanding, because the date you must
watch is **not** the one you might expect.

The registry has an **expiration date**. But CentralNic Reseller manages renewals
against its own **renewal date**, derived from the **paid-until date** plus a
TLD-specific **deletion timeframe**. For generic TLDs this pushes the renewal date
*after* the registry expiration date: gTLDs get a 45-day **auto-renew grace period
(ARGP)**, from which CentralNic Reseller subtracts 8 days for ICANN's Expired
Registration Recovery Policy and a 2-day safety buffer â€” leaving a renewal date
**35 days after the expiration date**. ccTLDs are not bound by ICANN and vary, so
treat 35 days as the gTLD case, not a universal constant.

:::caution[Watch the renewal date, not the expiration date]
The renewal date is the moment CentralNic Reseller will act â€” renew, expire or
delete the domain according to its renewal mode. It can fall well before or after
the registry's expiration date depending on the TLD. Any manual renewal should be
requested safely ahead of the renewal date (at least a day), and note that some
ccTLDs do **not** support explicit renewal at all â€” for those you extend the lease
by setting a "renew" renewal mode instead.
:::

Most renewal happens automatically, driven by the domain's **renewal mode**:

- **AutoRenew** â€” the domain renews itself at the renewal date. This is the account
  default and the safe choice for most resellers; it guards against accidental
  loss.
- **RenewOnce** â€” renew a single time, then revert to the account default. Useful
  when a TLD has no explicit-renewal command.
- **AutoExpire / AutoDelete** â€” do *not* renew; let the domain go (see
  [Expire](#expire-what-happens-when-you-do-nothing)).

Account-level modes set the default; per-domain settings override it. Explicit,
on-demand renewals use `RenewDomain`. The full mode list, the paid-until/renewal-date
model and the exceptions are all in
[The CentralNic Reseller Renewal System](../../../domains/renewal-system/).

:::caution[Auto-renew depends on funds]
CentralNic Reseller runs on prepayment, and an automated renewal can only complete
if the balance covers it. If funds are short at the renewal date, an AutoRenew
domain is **left to auto-expire instead** and removed â€” recovering it then means a
paid restore. Keep a working balance and a low-balance warning set.
:::

## Expire: what happens when you do nothing

Expiry is the *passive* end of the lifecycle â€” the lease ending because nothing
renewed it. It is not a single instant but a sequence, and understanding it is
what lets you recover a domain in time rather than discover it is gone.

For a gTLD left to expire, the domain does not vanish at the expiration date. It
first sits in the auto-renew grace period; during the ICANN ERRP portion of that
window it is suspended (it stops resolving and shows `pendingDelete`) so it is
unmistakable that the domain will be lost unless action is taken. Throughout this
phase a **renewal is still possible without a restore** â€” this is your last easy
off-ramp. Only when the renewal date passes with no renewal, and the mode says so,
does the domain actually get deleted.

Renewal mode decides the outcome at that point. **AutoExpire** and **AutoDelete**
both remove the domain from CentralNic Reseller at the end of the process; for
gTLDs they behave identically, but for some ccTLDs (for example `.DE` and `.AT`)
expiry and deletion trigger genuinely different registry processes â€” expiry can
hand the domain back to the registry for the registrant to manage directly. This
is a place to read the specific TLD's documentation rather than assume.

:::tip[Expiry is a window, not a cliff]
Between the expiration date and the actual deletion there is real time â€” 35 days
for gTLDs by the standard calculation â€” during which a straightforward renewal
still saves the domain. After deletion, the far more expensive restore path is
your only option. Watch the renewal date and act inside the window.
:::

## Restore: recovering a deleted domain

This is the stage that produces the most costly misunderstandings, so it gets the
clearest warning. Once a domain has been deleted â€” whether it expired or you
deleted it deliberately â€” it does **not** immediately become available for anyone
to register. gTLD registries hold it in a **redemption grace period (RGP)** of 30
days, shown as `redemptionPeriod`. During that window the original holder, and
only the original holder, can bring it back with a **restore**.

A restore (`RestoreDomain`, or `Domain:Restore` over EPP) is a distinct, billable
operation:

- It is **charged separately** from renewal, at a TLD-specific restore fee that is
  typically much higher than a registration.
- After an end-of-life restore, the domain is set to **RenewOnce** and an automatic
  renewal runs within minutes â€” so a restore usually costs **restore fee +
  renewal fee**. (A domain deleted mid-term is restored without that immediate
  renewal.) Do not change the renewal mode until that follow-up renewal has
  completed.
- Some ccTLDs and some restores need manual handling and are **not** processed in
  real time; you receive an event/poll message when they complete.

:::caution[Restore is not re-registration â€” this is the category error to avoid]
The most common and most expensive mistake developers make is treating a lapsed
domain like a fresh name: scripting a `CheckDomain` / `AddDomain` to "re-register"
it. During RGP the name is unavailable, so the check fails and the re-register
never happens â€” and worse, waiting for it to become "available" risks losing the
domain to someone else the moment RGP ends. To recover *your* deleted domain, use
**restore**, not register. They are different operations with different prices and
different outcomes.
:::

The RGP is finite. After it, the domain moves to a `pendingDelete` phase (about 5
days for gTLDs) during which it can no longer be restored, and then it is purged
from the registry and becomes available to the public again. One shortcut worth
knowing: deleting a domain *before* its expiration date sends it straight into RGP,
bypassing the auto-renew grace period. The restore rules, fees and ccTLD
exceptions are detailed in
[The CentralNic Reseller Renewal System](../../../domains/renewal-system/).

## Delete: ending the lease deliberately

Deletion is the *active* end of the lifecycle â€” you asking for the domain to be
removed (`DeleteDomain`, or `Domain:Delete` over EPP), rather than letting it
expire. It is a legitimate operation, but it is the sharpest edge on the whole map,
so treat it with care.

- **It is guarded by a lock for a reason.** A domain with `clientDeleteProhibited`
  or the registry's `serverDeleteProhibited` will refuse deletion until the lock is
  removed â€” a deliberate speed bump against accidental and malicious deletes.
- **It is not instant, and it is not free to undo.** As covered above, a delete
  (or a delete-before-expiry) sends the domain into the redemption grace period,
  from which only a paid restore recovers it. There is no "undo" that returns it to
  its prior state at no cost.
- **Prefer expiry to explicit deletion.** For letting go of unwanted domains, best
  practice is usually to set **AutoExpire** or **AutoDelete** and let the domain go
  at the natural end of its term, rather than deleting explicitly â€” it deletes at
  the latest safe moment and avoids surprises.

:::caution[Deletion is a one-way door with a paid escape hatch]
Automate deletes with the same caution you would give an irreversible production
change. Confirm the domain, respect the locks, and remember that the only route
back is a restore inside the redemption window â€” at the restore fee, not the
registration fee.
:::

## Reading the map: status codes tie it together

Every stage above surfaces as an EPP **status code**, and once the arc makes sense
the codes become a live readout of exactly where a domain sits:

- `ok` / `active` â€” settled, no pending operations or prohibitions.
- `inactive` â€” registered but no nameservers; not resolving.
- `pendingCreate`, `pendingTransfer`, `pendingRenew`, `pendingUpdate` â€” an
  operation is in flight and not yet final.
- `clientHold` / `serverHold` â€” deliberately not resolving (dispute, non-payment).
- `client*Prohibited` / `server*Prohibited` â€” locks against update, transfer or
  delete; `server*` (registry-set) outrank `client*` (registrar-set).
- `addPeriod`, `renewPeriod`, `autoRenewPeriod`, `transferPeriod` â€” informative
  grace windows after register, renew, auto-renew and transfer.
- `redemptionPeriod`, `pendingRestore`, `pendingDelete` â€” the end-of-life sequence:
  recoverable by restore, restore in progress, and past the point of no return.

When a domain misbehaves, read its status before you touch a command. The complete,
authoritative descriptions â€” including what action each code calls for â€” are in the
[EPP Status Codes](../../../domains/status/) reference.
